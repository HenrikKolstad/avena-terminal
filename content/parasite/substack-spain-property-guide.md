## Chapter 1: The Price Is Not the Price

The first thing you need to understand about Spanish new builds is that the listed price is the starting point of a negotiation, not the end of one.

Developers in Spain — especially those building on the Costa Blanca and Costa del Sol — are sitting on significant inventory. The post-pandemic construction boom produced a lot of units, and while demand from Northern European buyers is strong, it hasn't been strong enough to absorb everything.

What this means in practice: developers will deal. Not on every unit and not at every stage of construction, but the room for negotiation is real. I've seen discounts of 5-15% off list price, plus additional sweeteners like included furniture packages, upgraded kitchens, and covered transfer tax.

When I analyzed 1,867 new-build properties across Spain's coast, the average effective discount relative to comparable resale properties was 19%. That's not a typo. New builds, on average, are priced below what older apartments in the same postcodes are selling for.

This is counterintuitive. New builds are supposed to be more expensive. And in some markets they are. But in Spain in 2025, developer competition has created a pricing dynamic that favors buyers.

## Chapter 2: Location Granularity Matters More Than You Think

Everyone knows location matters. But in Spain, the granularity of that statement is extreme.

Two developments can be 2 kilometers apart and have completely different rental yield profiles. One might be walkable to the beach and near restaurants, producing 7-8% gross yield. The other might require a car for everything, dropping yields to 4-5%.

When I built the scoring model for what eventually became [Avena Terminal](https://avenaterminal.com), proximity to the coast was the single strongest predictor of rental performance. Not "coastal town" — actual walking distance to the water.

The second strongest predictor was walkability to daily amenities: supermarkets, restaurants, pharmacies. Properties that scored well on both metrics consistently outperformed.

This sounds obvious, but you'd be amazed how many buyers compromise on location because a development 3 kilometers inland offers a bigger terrace or an extra bedroom. The extra space doesn't generate rental income. The location does.

## Chapter 3: The Developer Question

Here's something agents rarely discuss in detail: the developer behind your property matters as much as the property itself.

In Spain, you're protected by law in several ways. The bank guarantee (aval bancario) protects your deposit payments if the developer goes bust. The 10-year structural warranty covers major defects. These are real protections and they work.

But they don't protect you from delays, poor finish quality, or the frustration of dealing with a developer who doesn't return calls after handover.

I tracked developer track records across the dataset and found a clear pattern: developers with 5+ completed projects in the last decade had significantly fewer complaints, better on-time delivery rates, and higher post-sale satisfaction. This isn't surprising, but it is useful. When you're choosing between two similar properties, pick the one built by the company with the proven track record.

## Chapter 4: The True Cost of Buying

The purchase price of a Spanish property is roughly 85-88% of what you'll actually spend. Here's where the rest goes:

**Transfer Tax (ITP) or VAT:** New builds carry 10% VAT plus 1.5% stamp duty in most regions. This is non-negotiable, although some developers will cover it as part of a deal.

**Legal Fees:** Budget 1-1.5% for a good independent lawyer. Do not use the developer's lawyer. I cannot stress this enough. Your lawyer should work for you and only you.

**Notary and Registry:** Around 0.5-1% combined.

**Furniture and Setup:** If the property doesn't come furnished, budget 5,000 to 15,000 euros depending on size and taste.

**Connection Fees:** Utility connections, internet setup, community fees for the first year. Usually 1,000-2,000 euros.

All in, expect to pay 12-15% on top of the purchase price. Anyone who tells you it's less is either misinformed or leaving something out.

## Chapter 5: The Yield Reality

I see a lot of optimistic yield projections from agents and developers. "8-10% gross yield" is thrown around constantly.

Here's what the data actually shows: across 1,867 properties, the average estimated gross yield was 6.2%. The top quartile — properties with the best locations and most efficient layouts for rental — averaged around 8%. The bottom quartile was closer to 4%.

After management fees (typically 15-25% of rental income for short-term lets), cleaning, maintenance, and vacancy periods, your net yield will be roughly 60-70% of the gross figure.

So a property with a 7% gross yield delivers around 4.5-5% net. That's still good — better than most European property markets — but it's not the 10% some people are being promised.

## Chapter 6: What I'd Actually Do

If I were buying a new build in Spain today, here's my playbook:

1. **Start with data, not viewings.** Before you get on a plane, understand which developments offer genuine value. Tools like [Avena Terminal](https://avenaterminal.com) let you compare properties on objective metrics rather than relying on glossy brochures.

2. **Set a maximum budget that includes all costs.** If your budget is 200,000 euros, your maximum property price is around 175,000 euros. Plan for the full 12-15% surcharge from day one.

3. **Prioritize walkable locations.** Beach proximity and walkable amenities drive rental income. Extra square meters in a car-dependent location do not.

4. **Research the developer obsessively.** Look up their previous projects. Drive past them. Talk to owners if you can. Check the building registry for any legal issues.

5. **Get an independent lawyer early.** Before you sign a reservation agreement. Before you pay a deposit. Before you fall in love with a property. Your lawyer reviews everything first.

6. **Don't rush.** Developers will tell you that units are selling fast. Sometimes they are. But the FOMO pitch is the oldest trick in property sales. If a deal is good today, it'll be good next week.

## The Honest Summary

Spain's new-build market in 2025 offers genuine value. The pricing dynamics favor buyers, the rental fundamentals are strong in the right locations, and the legal protections are robust by European standards.

But it's not a guaranteed win. Poor property selection, underestimating costs, or choosing the wrong developer can easily turn a good opportunity into an expensive headache.

The difference between a smart purchase and a regrettable one is information. The more you know before you commit, the better your outcome will be.
