# I Spent 6 Months Analyzing 1,800 Spanish Properties — Here's What I Found

*A data-driven deep dive into Spain's new-build market, and what the numbers actually say about value in 2025.*

---

I've always been skeptical of property marketing. Glossy renders, "luxury lifestyle" copy, infinity pool shots taken at golden hour — none of it tells you whether a property is actually worth buying.

So six months ago, I started doing something different. I began systematically collecting and analyzing data on every new-build development I could find across Spain's Mediterranean coast. Not opinions. Not vibes. Data.

By the time I stopped, I had a dataset of **1,867 properties** across Costa Blanca, Costa del Sol, and the Balearics. Here's what the numbers revealed.

## The Setup

I tracked every listing I could verify across major developers and agencies. For each property, I recorded the asking price, comparable resale values in the same postcode, build quality indicators, proximity to amenities, rental yield estimates, and developer track record.

Then I built a scoring model — a composite metric from 0 to 100 that weighs value-for-money against location fundamentals and rental potential. The idea was simple: strip away the marketing and see which properties are genuinely good deals and which are overpriced boxes with a sea view.

The tool I built to do this eventually became [Avena Terminal](https://avenaterminal.com), which is now publicly available. But more on that later — let's talk about what the data says.

## Finding #1: The Average Discount Is Larger Than You Think

Across the full dataset, the **average discount relative to comparable resale properties was 19%**. That surprised me. The common narrative is that new builds carry a premium because everything is shiny and new. And sometimes they do. But when you compare like-for-like — same postcode, same size, same distance to coast — new builds in many areas are priced below what older resale properties are fetching.

Why? Developers are competing aggressively for foreign buyers in 2025. They're absorbing costs, offering furniture packages, and in some cases covering transfer tax. The resale market, meanwhile, has tightened. Fewer owners are selling because mortgage rates have stabilized and nobody wants to give up a 1.5% fixed rate from 2021.

The result is a pricing inversion that most buyers don't realize exists.

## Finding #2: Yield Varies Wildly by Micro-Location

The **average estimated gross yield across all 1,867 properties was 6.2%**. Respectable. But that average hides enormous variation.

Properties within 500 meters of the coast in popular tourist zones (Torrevieja, Benidorm, Marbella) showed yields between 7% and 9%, driven by strong short-term rental demand. Move just 3 kilometers inland and yields drop to 4-5%, sometimes lower.

The lesson here is obvious but worth stating: in Spanish property, the micro-location matters more than the macro-location. A property in a less fashionable town but 200 meters from the beach will almost always outperform a property in a prestige postcode that's a 15-minute drive from the water.

## Finding #3: Most Properties Score Below Average

The **highest score in the dataset was 79 out of 100**. No property scored above 80. The median was 52.

This tells you something important: there are no perfect deals. Every property involves trade-offs. The ones scoring in the 70s had strong fundamentals — good price relative to comparables, solid rental potential, reputable developer — but they still had compromises. Maybe the community pool was small. Maybe the nearest supermarket was a 10-minute drive.

What the scoring model does well is separate the genuinely good from the mediocre. About 15% of properties scored above 65. These are the ones worth serious attention. The remaining 85% are either fairly priced (nothing special) or genuinely overpriced for what they deliver.

## Finding #4: Developer Reputation Is the Most Underrated Factor

I tracked which developers had a history of on-time delivery, which had construction quality complaints, and which had gone through financial restructuring. The correlation between developer reliability and long-term value retention was the strongest signal in the entire dataset.

Buyers obsess over kitchen finishes and terrace size. They should be obsessing over whether the developer will still exist in three years to honor the warranty.

## Finding #5: The "Best Time to Buy" Question Is Wrong

Everyone asks whether now is a good time to buy in Spain. The data suggests that timing matters far less than selection. The spread between the best-value properties and the worst-value properties in any given month was vastly larger than the price movement of the overall market across the six-month study period.

Put differently: picking the right property matters 10x more than picking the right month.

## What I'd Actually Do With This Information

If I were buying a new build in Spain today, I would focus on three things:

1. **Target the 19% discount zone.** Look for developments where new-build pricing sits meaningfully below resale comparables. This is your margin of safety.

2. **Prioritize walkable coast proximity.** The yield data is unambiguous. If rental income matters to you at all, proximity to the beach is the single biggest driver.

3. **Use data, not brochures.** The reason I built [Avena Terminal](https://avenaterminal.com) was that I couldn't find anything that scored properties objectively. Every comparison site was either an agency disguised as a portal or a portal with no analytical depth. ## The Bottom Line

Spain's new-build market in 2025 is not the no-brainer that some agents will tell you it is. But it's also not the minefield that pessimists describe. The data shows a market with genuine value — if you know where to look and, more importantly, if you have a framework for evaluating what "value" actually means beyond the price tag.

The properties that score well tend to share common traits: competitive pricing against resale, proximity to demand drivers, and developers with clean track records. Everything else is noise.
